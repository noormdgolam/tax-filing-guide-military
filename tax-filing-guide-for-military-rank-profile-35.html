Tax Filing Guide for Military Rank Profile 35

Published: July 15, 2026 • Written by Editorial Team

Congratulations on your military service, Military Rank Profile 35! Filing taxes can be a daunting task, but with this guide, you'll be well-prepared to navigate the process. We'll cover the essential information you need to know, including tax deductions, exemptions, and credits.

Tax Filing Status

Your tax filing status is determined by your marital status and eligibility for certain exemptions. As a member of the military, you may be eligible for the following filing statuses:

  • Single: If you're unmarried and do not claim any dependents.
  • Head of Household: If you're unmarried or considered unmarried on the last day of the tax year, have a qualifying person, and meet certain residence and filing requirements.
  • Married Filing Jointly: If you're married and file your tax return with your spouse.

Allowances and Dependents

As a member of the military, you may be eligible for certain allowances that can be deducted on your tax return. These allowances include:

  • Basis Allowance**: For housing expenses
  • Subsistence Allowance**: For food and other living expenses not covered by the basis allowance
  • BAQ (Basic Allowance for Quarters)**: For housing expenses, especially when not provided with military quarters
  • Family Separation Allowance**: For being separated from your family for military reasons
  • Rubber Use Allowance**: For the wear and tear on personal vehicles caused by long deployments or temporary duty assignments

Deductions and Credits

The military offers various tax deductions and credits that can help reduce your taxable income. Some examples include:

  • Tuition Assistance Deduction**: A maximum deduction of up to $4,000 for qualified higher education expenses.
  • Military Savings Deduction**: A deduction for deposits placed in military savings accounts, such as the Military Savings Plan.
  • Military Child Care Assistance Credit**: A credit for eligible military child care expenses.
  • Disabled Veteran Tax Credit**: A credit of up to $5,000 for disabled veterans, or up to $10,000 if the veteran was severely disabled.

Important Tax Forms

The IRS has specific tax forms for military personnel to report their income and claim deductions and credits. Make sure to review and file the following forms:

  • Form 1040**: The standard form for individual federal income tax returns.
  • Form 8596**: The form for calculating the Military Savings Deduction.
  • Form 8880**: The form for claiming the Child Tax Credit.
  • Form W-2**:** The form for reporting wage and tax information, which the military personnel must obtain from their commanding officer.

Extensions and Due Dates

The IRS deadline for filing individual federal income tax returns (Form 1040) is typically April 15th. Military personnel who require more time to prepare their tax return may apply for an automatic extension using Form 4868. The extension request must be submitted by April 15th, allowing an extra six months to file, or by October 15th.

Professional Assistance

Seek assistance from a tax professional if you need help preparing your tax return. This is particularly recommended if your situation involves complex military benefits or exemptions. They can provide valuable guidance and help you make the most of the tax savings available to you.
