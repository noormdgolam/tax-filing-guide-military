Tax Filing Guide for Military Rank Profile 34

Published: July 15, 2026 • Written by Editorial Team

Military personnel assigned to Rank Profile 34 often navigate complex tax situations due to their unique work arrangements and tax exemptions.

Individuals assigned to Rank Profile 34 are usually Non-Commissioned Officers (NCOs) holding specific ranks such as Staff Sergeant (SSG), Sergeant First Class (SFC) or Master Sergeant (MSG). This rank profile is often associated with leadership roles within units and typically involves a wide range of duties from command to tactical operations.

To ensure accurate and compliant tax filing, it is essential to understand the tax implications arising from military service. Here are a few key considerations to explore:

  • Tax Exemptions: As a member of the U.S. Armed Forces, personnel assigned to Rank Profile 34 are entitled to certain tax exemptions, such as the Basic Allowance for Housing (BAH) and the Basic Allowance for SUBSISTENCE (BAS). The IRS designates these benefits as 'tax-free.'
  • Combat Zone Differential (CZD): Members serving in combat zones are generally exempt from paying certain federal taxes, such as Social Security and Medicare. Additionally, CZD provides an additional amount to compensate service members for paying federal income tax on the extra pay or allowances they receive while serving in combat zones.
  • Tax Credits: Service members may be eligible for tax credits such as the Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC), depending on their filing status, income level, and eligibility requirements. Additionally, the American Opportunity Tax Credit (AOTC) or the Child Tax Credit (CTC) may be available to military families.
  • Combat Theater Differential Pay: This pay is a taxable income component and needs to be factored into annual tax calculations. Service members should claim Combat Theater Pay on their tax returns, usually as Ordinary Income.
  • Relocation Expenses: If personnel move frequently for work duties, they may be entitled to deduct moving expenses, provided they meet specific requirements. IRS Form 3903, Moving Expenses, can guide the process.

Military personnel assigned to Rank Profile 34 should also consider the following documentation when filing taxes:

  • DOD Form 214 - DD Form 214: A Certificate of Release or Discharge from Active Duty, which is used to claim tax credits and benefits.
  • W-2 forms from military pay and allowances. These forms will outline total income, combat zone pay, and tax withheld.
  • 1099-R forms, which may be issued for the refund or exchange of education savings, such as a Thrift Savings Plan (TSP),
  • Form 1040A or 1040: The primary forms for federal income tax returns, which are filled out to claim tax credits and report income. Military personnel should consult tax software or seek professional advice for optimal tax filing.
  • Form 8938 and Form 3520: These forms need to be completed for non-U.S. assets, and tax-exempt income, respectively.

Military personnel who experience challenges while filing their tax returns or have concerns regarding the information above should consult relevant tax professionals, such as a CPA or an enrolled agent, who possess the knowledge required to handle tax issues related to military service.

Additionally, the U.S. Department of the Treasury, Internal Revenue Service (IRS) offers support and guidance via its website (irs.gov) for all military service members.

Maintaining detailed documentation of tax-related financial information and understanding relevant tax benefits helps ensure that military personnel assigned to Rank Profile 34 can accurately complete their federal income tax returns and make informed tax decisions throughout their careers.
