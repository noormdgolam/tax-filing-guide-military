Tax Filing Guide for Military Rank Profile 72

Military Rank Profile 72 refers to the tax filing designation for members of the Armed Forces who have active duty status in the Navy. These personnel may be entitled to various tax benefits and considerations due to their military service.

Key Tax Filing Considerations

Members of Military Rank Profile 72 are considered active duty military personnel and may be eligible for the Combat Zone Exclusion, the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion, and other tax benefits. In addition, spouses and dependents of active duty personnel may also be eligible for tax benefits such as the Combat Zone Exclusion and the Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC).

Active duty personnel are also exempt from state income tax in their state of origin, and may be exempt from state tax in the state where they are stationed, depending on the state's tax laws.

Tax Return Filing Status

Military personnel who are single or married filing a joint return may claim their spouse as an exempt individual on their tax return. Married couples that file separate returns are still considered married when determining their filing status, regardless of the military member's active duty status.

Dependent parents are considered dependents of the active duty member, and may be eligible for the Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) or the Child Tax Credit, among other tax benefits.

Tax Filing Obligations

Members of Military Rank Profile 72 must file Form 1040 and attach Form 8853, which provides tax benefits to members of the Armed Forces stationed in Combat Zones. Additionally, active duty personnel must also attach Form 2555, which applies the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion to foreign earnings.

Active duty personnel who are not residing in the United States may be eligible for the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE) and do not need to file a tax return if their foreign earnings do not exceed a certain amount.

Special Tax Situations

Active duty personnel who are residents of Alaska, California, Colorado, Florida, Nevada, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Utah, Washington, and Wyoming may be exempt from state tax due to their state of origin or where they are stationed. Members from other states should check with their state's tax authority to see if they are exempt from state income tax.

Spouses of active duty personnel who are residents of a combat zone may be eligible for tax benefits, including the Combat Zone Exclusion and the Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC).

Penalties and Interest

Servicemembers are exempt from late filing, payment, and penalty and interest on tax debt for reasonable errors and omissions in their tax returns. This applies when a tax return or payment is late due to military service, deployment, or other circumstances.

It is recommended that taxpayers consult with a qualified tax professional or the Internal Revenue Service to determine their specific tax obligations and benefits.

  • Form 8853, Combat Zone Exclusion and/or Earned Income Exclusion for Combat Zone Residents
