Tax Filing Guide for Military Rank Profile 62

Published: July 15, 2026 • Written by Editorial Team

The IRS acknowledges unique filing requirements for active-duty military personnel serving in remote or high-risk assignments, specifically those ranked 62. To facilitate a seamless tax filing process, we have compiled an updated guide, taking into account the Military Spouses Residency Relief Act (MSRRA) and other pertinent military tax considerations.

Individuals ranked 62 typically find themselves stationed in areas prone to frequent deployments and assignments. As a result, maintaining a permanent residency status might be challenging. In this context, it's crucial to familiarize yourselves with the MSRRA provisions, which effectively address the residency and domicile concerns of military spouses.

Key considerations for tax filing as an E-6 (Rank 62):

  • Domicile vs. Residency: In your tax filing, domicile refers to the place you consider your permanent home, where you have ties, property, and other connections. Residency, on the other hand, pertains to the location where you have temporary or short-term residence.
  • Filing Status and Exemptions: Service members and their spouses may claim the standard deduction or itemize deductions, depending on their tax situation. The earned income tax credit (EITC) may be claimed by service members or spouses with earned income, while dependent exemptions are granted for qualifying dependents.
  • Combat Zone Exclusion: Income earned in combat zones may be tax-free or reduced depending on your filing status and the duration of your deployment. Ensure you claim any applicable exclusion to avoid overpayment of taxes.
  • State & Foreign Tax Credits: If stationed abroad or have moved between residences within different states, keep records of state income taxes paid or any foreign taxes on earned income. You may be eligible for state and foreign tax credits to avoid double taxation.
  • Military Benefits & Relocation Assistance: Certain relocation benefits, e.g., moving costs and BAH (Basic Allowance for Housing), do not qualify as earned income but can impact your tax return. Familiarize yourself with these benefits to report them accurately and claim any applicable deductions.
  • Form 8938 & FATCA: U.S. citizen and resident service members who own foreign financial assets above the required threshold (typically $50,000 or $75,000 for joint filers) must file Form 8938 to comply with the Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (FATCA).
  • Extension Request: Given the complexities in calculating earned income, deductions, and exemptions, you may want to consider filing for an extension if necessary. Ensure you have all supporting documentation and consult a tax professional if needed.

Military personnel and their spouses often encounter unique taxation scenarios. Adhering to MSRRA guidelines and understanding your specific filing obligations will significantly simplify your tax preparation process. It is highly recommended that you seek professional assistance from a qualified tax preparer to optimize your tax savings and minimize potential complications.
