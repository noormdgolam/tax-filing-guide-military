Hawaii Military Tax Filing Basics

Military personnel and their families often face unique financial challenges due to relocation, deployments, and other aspects of military life. However, understanding the basics of Hawaii tax laws can help alleviate some of this burden. Here are some essential key points for military personnel and their families to consider when filing taxes in Hawaii.

Tax Year and Filing Deadline

The federal tax year runs from January 1 to December 31, while the Hawaii tax year typically coincides with the federal tax year. Therefore, federal and state tax returns are often due on the same day, April 15th. However, those who file for an extension have an October 15th deadline to file both federal and Hawaii state tax returns.

Deductible Expenses

As members of the U.S. armed forces, military personnel and their families may qualify for various deductions, including:

  • Moving Expenses: Reimbursement for qualified moving expenses, including transportation, housing, and storage.
  • Meals and Lodging: Reimbursement for meals and lodging during temporary duty (TDY) and deployments.
  • Thrift Savings Plan (TSP) Contributions: Contributions made to the TSP are deductible, regardless of where the money originated.
  • Home Mortgage Interest and Property Taxes: Mortgage interest and property taxes may be deductible for homeowners, even if they've been stationed overseas.

As a state, Hawaii does not tax military pay, unless it's earned from non-federal sources. Military personnel and their families must consider the following tax laws in Hawaii:

  • Residency Status: Service members may have to file as a non-resident if stationed in Hawaii for less than 6 months. However, if stationed for more than 6 months, they're likely considered a Hawaii resident and must file their taxes as a state resident.
  • Personal Income Tax: Hawaii levies an income tax rate ranging from 8.25% to 11% on non-exempt income, depending on your income level.
  • Spouse and Dependent Exemptions: In Hawaii, a service member's family is eligible for various exemptions, including personal exemptions and dependency exemptions.

Tax Credits and Other Benefits

Military personnel and their families may qualify for various tax credits, including the earned income tax credit (EITC) if their income falls below a certain threshold. Furthermore, they may be eligible for:

  • Uniform Subsistence Allowance (USA) or Basic Allowance for Subsistence (BAS): Military personnel may be exempt from paying taxes on their USA or BAS income.
  • Student Loan Forgiveness: Student loan forgiveness offered through the military may lead to excluded income for tax purposes.

Additional Resources

For more information on Hawaii tax laws and military-specific tax benefits, visit the Hawaii Department of Taxation website or consult with a licensed tax professional to ensure accurate and timely tax filing and to address specific questions or concerns.

Key Rules and Regulations

When dealing with IRS guidelines regarding military service, it's crucial to consult the latest version of IRS Publication 3 (Armed Forces' Tax Guide). The general rules stipulate that:

  • Always maintain accurate records of your LES.
  • Understand the difference between taxable and non-taxable income (like BAH and BAS).
  • Know your state of legal residency (SLR) versus your physical duty station.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this applicable to Reservists and National Guard?

Yes, but the rules can vary depending on whether you are activated on Title 10 orders or performing weekend drills. Travel expenses for drills over 100 miles from home may have different deductible statuses.

Does this affect my spouse?

Under the Military Spouse Residency Relief Act (MSRRA), spouses have specific protections regarding where they pay state income tax, which directly intertwines with this topic.
