Home Sale Tax Exclusion for Military Families

Military personnel and their families often face unique challenges, including frequent relocation and deployment. One financial concern that may weigh on the minds of military families is the tax implications of selling their primary residence. Fortunately, the federal government provides an exemption for military personnel to exclude capital gains tax on the sale of their main home, as long as certain requirements are met.

Election Under IRC Section 121

Under the Internal Revenue Code (IRC) Section 121, also known as the main home exclusion, military families can exclude up to $250,000 ($500,000 for joint filers) of capital gains tax on the sale of their primary residence. To qualify, the seller must meet two tests: the primary residence test and the use test.

The primary residence requirement states that the property must be the seller's primary residence, which includes active duty military members, veterans, and surviving spouses of members. However, in certain cases, military personnel may need to transfer to a new permanent duty station, in which they must relocate to the new residence or transfer the old home into a family trust.

  • The primary residence must be owned and used by the seller as their main home for at least two of the five years preceding the sale.
  • The primary residence does not have to be the seller's principal residence for the entire five years, but at least two of those years must be.
  • Exceptions exist for disabled military personnel and those who died on active duty.

Using the Exclusion

To take advantage of the exclusion, sellers must complete and attach Form 121, Exclusion of Gain on Sale of Primary Residence, and Schedule D, Capital Gains and Losses, to their tax return. Military personnel should report the full sale amount and claim this exclusion by filing Form 8859, Qualifying Child Care Expenses of Qualifying Children of the Exempts.

Other Important Considerations

While the exclusion is an attractive benefit for military families, certain aspects must be considered. For example, if the gain on the sale of the home exceeds the allowed exclusion, the remaining profit will be subject to capital gains tax. Additionally, the exclusion is limited to the seller's main home and only counts gains from the sale of one home in a lifetime. Military personnel and their families should consult with a qualified tax professional to determine whether the exclusion applies to their specific situation.

Navigating Complexity

Selling a home for military personnel can be complex and tax implications can vary, especially in cases of deployment or divorce. To get the best guidance and tax planning strategies, it's best to consult with an experienced tax professional and review their qualifications on their website.

Key Rules and Regulations

When dealing with IRS guidelines regarding military service, it's crucial to consult the latest version of IRS Publication 3 (Armed Forces' Tax Guide). The general rules stipulate that:

  • Always maintain accurate records of your LES.
  • Understand the difference between taxable and non-taxable income (like BAH and BAS).
  • Know your state of legal residency (SLR) versus your physical duty station.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this applicable to Reservists and National Guard?

Yes, but the rules can vary depending on whether you are activated on Title 10 orders or performing weekend drills. Travel expenses for drills over 100 miles from home may have different deductible statuses.

Does this affect my spouse?

Under the Military Spouse Residency Relief Act (MSRRA), spouses have specific protections regarding where they pay state income tax, which directly intertwines with this topic.
