California Military Tax Filing Basics

Military personnel and their families stationed in California must file state tax returns, in addition to federal tax returns. Understanding California tax filing basics will help them navigate the process and minimize tax liabilities.

California state tax laws exempt military personnel stationed in the state from many tax obligations, but they still need to file state tax returns. Here's what you need to know:

California Tax Exemptions for Military Personnel

California provides various tax exemptions and credits for military personnel, including:

  • Exemption of military compensation, including base pay, housing allowance, and basic allowance for subsistence from state income tax
  • Exemption of federal tax refunds, including combat pay, hazard pay, and combat zone bonus from state tax
  • Exemption of state tax withholding for military personnel with dependents
  • Availability of the Military Residency Exception for military personnel stationed in California

Residency and Filing Status

In California, military personnel and their family members can claim either California residency or non-residency for tax purposes. If you do not claim permanent California residency, California considers you a non-resident and limits your tax liability to income earned in the state. You'll need to file a Form 540NR, California Nonresident or Part-Year Resident Income Tax Return.

However, if you claim California residency, you'll file a Form 540, California Resident Income Tax Return. To maintain California residency, you must:

  • Be physically present in the state for at least 6 months of the tax year
  • Intend to return to the state after an out-of-state move or travel
  • Maintain a permanent home or address in the state

Tax Credits for Military Personnel

California offers the following tax credits to military personnel:

  • California Military Residency Exception: You can claim this credit if you're a resident of California and stationed outside the state for military duties, but you still maintain a home in California.
  • California Military Personnel's Military Spouses' Education Credit: You may be eligible for this credit if you're stationed in a combat zone for at least 90 days or are a Purple Heart recipient.

How to File in California

To file a tax return in California, you'll need to gather necessary documents, including:

  • W-2 and 1099 forms
  • Military orders, pay stubs, and base housing documents
  • Dependent care records, if applicable

You may e-file your tax return through the Franchise Tax Board (FTB) website or use tax preparation software to guide you through the process. Consult the FTB website or a tax professional for more information on California military tax filing requirements and deadlines.

Key Rules and Regulations

When dealing with IRS guidelines regarding military service, it's crucial to consult the latest version of IRS Publication 3 (Armed Forces' Tax Guide). The general rules stipulate that:

  • Always maintain accurate records of your LES.
  • Understand the difference between taxable and non-taxable income (like BAH and BAS).
  • Know your state of legal residency (SLR) versus your physical duty station.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this applicable to Reservists and National Guard?

Yes, but the rules can vary depending on whether you are activated on Title 10 orders or performing weekend drills. Travel expenses for drills over 100 miles from home may have different deductible statuses.

Does this affect my spouse?

Under the Military Spouse Residency Relief Act (MSRRA), spouses have specific protections regarding where they pay state income tax, which directly intertwines with this topic.
