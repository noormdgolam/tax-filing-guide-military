Idaho Military Tax Filing Basics

Military personnel and their families often face unique tax situations due to their military service. Understanding Idaho tax laws and federal tax laws will help simplify the tax filing process. This article covers the basics of Idaho military tax filing and how to take advantage of tax benefits.

Idaho State Tax Filing Requirements

As an Idaho resident, you're required to file a state income tax return, even if you're exempt from federal taxes. You'll need to provide your Social Security number, ID, and military ID (if applicable) to file your tax return.

Tax Exemptions for Military Personnel

Idaho has a 6.925% state income tax rate, but military personnel with a W-2 or 1099 from a job outside Idaho may be exempt from state income tax. Additionally, non-resident Idahoans with a military pension or retirement income from an Idaho source may need to file an Idaho tax return. Consult a tax professional to determine your tax situation.

Federal Tax Considerations

The federal government offers unique tax benefits for military personnel, such as the Combat Zone Tax Exclusion and the Servicemembers Civil Relief Act (SCRA). However, federal tax filing deadlines may vary, so ensure you understand the different deadlines.

Combat Zone Tax Exclusion

The Combat Zone Tax Exclusion allows military personnel serving in combat zones to exclude certain types of income, such as hazardous duty pay, from federal taxation. Additionally, you may exclude up to $500,000 in Combat Pay, and $300,000 if you're not single or the head of household. If you receive hazardous duty pay from the state of Idaho, it's not taxed by Idaho but you may have received a W2 and that's why there might be a need for a tax filing.

Military Family Tax Benefits

Family members may qualify for various tax benefits, such as the Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) or education credits like the American Opportunity Tax Credit. Dependents of military personnel with special combat zone status may receive tax credits or exemptions.

Tax Credits for Dependent Care

Military families can claim the Dependent Care Credit for childcare or other expenses related to caring for a child or elderly dependent. This credit can be claimed on Form 1040, even if you don't itemize deductions.

Free Tax Assistance for Military Personnel

Take advantage of free tax services provided by the IRS, such as the Voluntary Income Tax Assistance (VITA) program or the Tax Counseling for the Elderly (TCE) program. You can also visit a local Military OneSource office for more information.

Idaho Military Tax Resources

For specific Idaho tax information, consult the Idaho State Tax Commission website or contact the Idaho Military Tax office. They can provide answers to state tax questions specific to military personnel.

Tax Terms for Military Personnel

Here are some tax terms to know as a military personnel:

  • Combat Zone Tax Exclusion: A tax exclusion for income earned while serving in a combat zone.
  • Servicemembers Civil Relief Act (SCRA): A federal law offering tax relief and protection to military personnel called to active duty.
  • Combat Pay: Hazardous duty pay received for serving in a combat zone.
  • Exclusion: The elimination of a certain type of income from taxation.

Key Rules and Regulations

When dealing with IRS guidelines regarding military service, it's crucial to consult the latest version of IRS Publication 3 (Armed Forces' Tax Guide). The general rules stipulate that:

  • Always maintain accurate records of your LES.
  • Understand the difference between taxable and non-taxable income (like BAH and BAS).
  • Know your state of legal residency (SLR) versus your physical duty station.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this applicable to Reservists and National Guard?

Yes, but the rules can vary depending on whether you are activated on Title 10 orders or performing weekend drills. Travel expenses for drills over 100 miles from home may have different deductible statuses.

Does this affect my spouse?

Under the Military Spouse Residency Relief Act (MSRRA), spouses have specific protections regarding where they pay state income tax, which directly intertwines with this topic.
