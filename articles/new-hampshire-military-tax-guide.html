New Hampshire Military Tax Filing Basics

Military personnel and their families often face unique challenges when filing taxes, including navigating complex tax laws and ensuring they receive the benefits they deserve. New Hampshire, in particular, has several tax filing basics that military personnel and their families should be aware of.

First and foremost, New Hampshire has no state income tax, making it a desirable location for military personnel and their families to live while serving their country. However, this also means that military personnel may need to file for other tax credits and deductions that are available to them.

  • No State Income Tax:

    • New Hampshire does not have a state income tax, which means military personnel will not pay state income tax on their wages. This can be beneficial for military personnel who are stationed in New Hampshire on a permanent change of station (PCS) order.

  • State Tax-Free Income:

    • New Hampshire only taxes interest and dividend income above $2,500. Therefore, military personnel and their families with interest and dividend income may be subject to a 5% tax on their income above this threshold.

Military Tax Filing Benefits

The IRS offers several tax filing benefits for military personnel and their families. These benefits include:

  • Military Basic Allowance for Housing (BAH):

    • The IRS considers housing benefits such as BAH as nontaxable income. This benefits military personnel who may receive a housing stipend as part of their military pay.

  • Combat Pay:

    • Combat pay, also known as hazardous duty pay, is nontaxable income and should not be reported on a tax return. Military personnel who receive combat pay can exclude it from their gross income on their tax return.

  • Move Expenses:

    • Military personnel and their families may qualify for a moving expense deduction, also known as a move reimbursement, for costs related to relocating for military orders.

  • Reserve Component Personnel:

    • Reserve component personnel may qualify for a tax credit for certain military-related education expenses.

Tax Credit for Military Service

Military personnel who have been deployed to combat zones or are serving in certain areas may be eligible for a tax credit, known as the Combat Zone Tax Relief Act (CZTRA).

In addition, military personnel who are permanently stationed in areas with high living costs, such as Germany or Japan, may be eligible for a tax credit known as the Exclusion of Combat Pay from Income. This credit allows military personnel to exclude combat pay from their gross income on their tax return.

Resources for Military Tax Filers

Military personnel and their families may need to seek additional resources and guidance when filing taxes. Some recommended resources include:

  • IRS Publication 3 (2019): Armed Forces' Tax Guide

    • The IRS publishes an annual guide specifically for military personnel and their families. This publication provides detailed information on tax benefits and credits available to military personnel.

  • Military OneSource:

    • Military OneSource is a comprehensive resource for military personnel and their families, offering tax-related support, as well as other benefits and resources.

  • Local IRS Office:

    • Local IRS offices and tax preparation offices may have tax professionals with experience in military tax filing. These experts can provide personalized guidance and support.

  • Online Resources:

    • Online resources, such as the Military OneSource website and the IRS website, also provide valuable information and resources for military tax filing.

By understanding New Hampshire's tax laws and the tax benefits available to military personnel, military personnel and their families can ensure they receive the benefits they deserve and file their taxes correctly.

Tax Forms Specific to New Hampshire Military Personnel

New Hampshire military personnel must complete the following tax forms:

  • Form 1040-NR: Income Tax Return for Nonresident Alien Individuals

    • This form is specifically designed for nonresident aliens who receive income from a U.S. source.

  • Form 4137: Social Security and Medicare Tax on Unreported Tip Income

    • Some military personnel may be required to report tip income on this form.

Conclusion

In conclusion, New Hampshire military personnel and their families face a unique set of tax challenges. Understanding the basics of New Hampshire taxation and military tax benefits is crucial for filing taxes correctly and maximizing available benefits.

Additional resources, such as IRS publications, Military OneSource, and local tax professionals, can provide valuable support and guidance when filing taxes.

By being informed and taking advantage of available tax benefits, military personnel and their families can ensure they receive the compensation and benefits they have earned.

Key Rules and Regulations

When dealing with IRS guidelines regarding military service, it's crucial to consult the latest version of IRS Publication 3 (Armed Forces' Tax Guide). The general rules stipulate that:

  • Always maintain accurate records of your LES.
  • Understand the difference between taxable and non-taxable income (like BAH and BAS).
  • Know your state of legal residency (SLR) versus your physical duty station.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this applicable to Reservists and National Guard?

Yes, but the rules can vary depending on whether you are activated on Title 10 orders or performing weekend drills. Travel expenses for drills over 100 miles from home may have different deductible statuses.

Does this affect my spouse?

Under the Military Spouse Residency Relief Act (MSRRA), spouses have specific protections regarding where they pay state income tax, which directly intertwines with this topic.
