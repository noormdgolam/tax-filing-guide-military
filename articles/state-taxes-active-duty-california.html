State Taxes for Active Duty Military in California

Military personnel serving in California are subject to state income tax, but they may be eligible for certain exemptions and deductions. Understanding these tax laws can help active-duty military personnel and their families in California minimize their tax liability.

Eligibility for Exemptions

To qualify for an exemption, military personnel must meet specific requirements:

  • They must be on active duty for more than 179 days in the tax year (or in the entire tax year for those on a full-time reserve duty) to qualify for a partial exemption (see below).
  • For the 2022 tax year, the total California income tax withheld will be completely exempt for service in California or outside the state while on duty and for service inside or outside California under certain circumstances (see below).

Partial Exemption

Active-duty military personnel who meet the requirements (179 days of service, with some exceptions) qualify for a partial exemption on their state income tax. This exemption is calculated based on the total amount of military housing allowance (MHA) received:

  • MHA is exempt from taxation up to $18,500 for the 2022 tax year
  • Only if total California income tax liability is greater than MHA, a partial exemption will be applied

Taxable Income

Military personnel who receive a California MHA or other income are required to report and pay state taxes, just like civilians:

  • California MHA (up to $18,500 exempt, but above this, MHA remains fully taxable)
  • Combat pay (taxable)
  • Base pay (taxable)
  • Benefits and allowances received outside California (may be non-taxable)

Deductions for Military Personnel

Military personnel serving in California may be eligible for special deductions:

  • The deduction for certain moving expenses to or from a base in California to avoid paying state income taxes on income received for a short period, prior to receiving tax-exempt income or moving to a duty station outside of California
  • Military personnel who are California residents while living off-base may be able to claim a home office deduction if the residence doubles as an office

Military Tax Credits

Active-duty military personnel are eligible for credits on their state taxes:

  • Credit of 10% off the minimum tax liability to all active duty military personnel and their spouses

Special Tax Situations

Special tax considerations and exemptions apply to military personnel in California:

  • The spouse of the California serviceperson is exempt from taxes if they meet the requirements in California
  • The tax liability may differ depending on the status of being part time (short-time enlistment), and active duty for a full-time or part-time enlistment

Consult the California Franchise Tax Board and a tax expert if questions arise regarding state taxes in California for active-duty members, spouses, and dependents.

Key Rules and Regulations

When dealing with IRS guidelines regarding military service, it's crucial to consult the latest version of IRS Publication 3 (Armed Forces' Tax Guide). The general rules stipulate that:

  • Always maintain accurate records of your LES.
  • Understand the difference between taxable and non-taxable income (like BAH and BAS).
  • Know your state of legal residency (SLR) versus your physical duty station.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this applicable to Reservists and National Guard?

Yes, but the rules can vary depending on whether you are activated on Title 10 orders or performing weekend drills. Travel expenses for drills over 100 miles from home may have different deductible statuses.

Does this affect my spouse?

Under the Military Spouse Residency Relief Act (MSRRA), spouses have specific protections regarding where they pay state income tax, which directly intertwines with this topic.
