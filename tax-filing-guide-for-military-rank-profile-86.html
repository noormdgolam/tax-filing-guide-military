Tax Filing Guide for Military Rank Profile 86

The Internal Revenue Service (IRS) recognizes the unique challenges and benefits faced by members of the military. To support these service members, the IRS has implemented various tax policies, including automatic extensions of tax filing deadlines and special rules for tax filing status.

Tax Filing Status

As a member of the military with a rank of E-5 (Specialist/Corporal) and a profile number 86, your tax filing status may be different than that of civilian counterparts. Your tax filing status is determined by your marital status and number of dependents.

  • Single Military Personnel: If you are unmarried and have no dependents, your tax filing status is single. You will file as a single taxpayer and claim the standard deduction.
  • : If you are married, your tax filing status will depend on your spouse's status and the number of dependents you have. You will file jointly as single or head of household if your spouse has no income or a low income.
  • Head of Household: If you are unmarried with dependents, such as children, you may be eligible for head of household filing status. This can result in lower tax rates and larger deductions.

Tax Deductions and Credits

Members of the military are eligible for various tax deductions and credits to reduce their taxable income and lower their tax liability. Some of these benefits include:

  • Military Deduction: The military deduction provides a standard deduction of $20,000 ($13,000 for single personnel) for eligible military personnel. You do not need to itemize deductions to take advantage of this.
  • Military Tax Credit: The military tax credit is a non-refundable credit worth $250 for single personnel and $500 for joint filers. This credit is based on your basic pay and not on deployed income.
  • Combat Zone Benefits: If you are a member of the military and serving in a combat zone, you may be entitled to various tax credits and deductions, such as the tax-free combat zone pay and the exemption of up to $90,000 of combat zone income from tax.

Automatic Extensions and Special Filing Rules

Military personnel often face unique challenges when filing their taxes, such as deployed locations and short deadlines. To accommodate the needs of military personnel, the IRS has implemented automatic extensions of tax filing deadlines and special filing rules, including:

  • Automatic Filings for Joint Military Servicemembers, automatic filing extension for joint filers with at least one member serving in combat or contingency operations or is deployed in connection with combat or contingency operations for more than 330 days.

Common Forms for Military Personnel

When preparing your tax return, you may need to complete various forms, including:

  • Form W-2 and Form W-2AS: To report military compensation, including basic pay, allowances, and non-taxable combat pay.
  • Form 1099-MISC: To report military income, such as hazardous duty pay, jump pay, and flight pay.
  • Form 2106: To claim the military deduction and other military tax benefits.
  • , Form to report additional Tax Information on Specified Foreign Financial Assets.

Tax Professionals and IRS Resources

If you are unsure about your tax filing status or are struggling to prepare your tax return, you can consider consulting a professional tax preparer who is familiar with military tax issues. You can also reach out to the IRS for additional guidance, including:

  • IRS Publication 3: Armed Forces' Tax Guide.
  • IRS Video on Military Taxation: Provides information on military tax benefits, including the military deduction and combat zone pay.
